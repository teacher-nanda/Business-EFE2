Part 2 · Vocabulary
Vocabulary — Reporting & Communication
10 min
📝 Key vocabulary
I need to brief the team on what was decided in this morning's meeting.
to brief someone
[to give someone a summary of key information]
Could you pass on the message to Sarah? She wasn't in the meeting.
to pass on (information)
[to communicate something to someone else]
He claimed the figures had already been verified by the finance team.
to claim
[to state something as a fact, especially without proof]
She admitted that the project was behind schedule.
to admit
[to confess or acknowledge something, often reluctantly]
The manager warned us that the deadline could not be moved.
to warn
[to tell someone about a potential problem or risk]
The chair suggested that we postpone the decision until next week.
to suggest
[to propose an idea for others to consider]

Part 3 · Key Language
From Direct to Reported Speech
10 min

When you report what someone said, the verb tense usually shifts one step back in time.

Direct speech
Reported speech
"I work from home." (present simple)
→
She said she worked from home. (past simple)
"We are reviewing the data." (present continuous)
→
He said they were reviewing the data. (past continuous)
"I will send the report." (will)
→
She said she would send the report. (would)
"I can attend the meeting." (can)
→
He said he could attend the meeting. (could)
"We have finished the proposal." (present perfect)
→
They said they had finished the proposal. (past perfect)
"I sent the email yesterday." (past simple)
→
She said she had sent the email the day before. (past perfect or past simple)

Find the mistake — one error per sentence, no clues Error correction
"She said me that the meeting would be postponed until Friday."
"said me" → "She told me that…" — "tell" needs an object (tell someone). "Say" does not: "She said that…"
"She said she will send the report tomorrow."
Two changes needed: "She said she would send the report the following day." — "will" → "would"; "tomorrow" → "the following day".
"He asked me where does the nearest office is located."
Reported question word order: "He asked me where the nearest office was located." — No inversion in reported questions; verb goes back one tense.

Part 4 · Guided Speaking
Scenario: The Debrief
15 min
Your role
Ana — you attended the budget meeting
Reporting to
Your manager, David (who was absent)
Your task
Brief David on everything discussed and decided
📌 Useful phrases to keep using
"The aim of today's meeting is to…" — stating the purpose (useful source phrase to report)
"I'd like to hand over to [name] to cover the next point." — managing the floor (useful source phrase to report)
"Could we come back to that in a moment?" — parking a point politely
"To summarize what we've agreed so far…" — summarising during the debrief
Situation 1 of 6
1 Opening the debrief
David stops Ana in the corridor: "So, how did the budget meeting go? I couldn't make it."
💬 How does Ana open her debrief — naturally but professionally?
💡 Set the context: who was there, what the purpose was, overall tone
Sample answer
"It was a productive meeting. The main objective was to agree on next quarter's budget. Mark chaired it, and most of the team was there — except Pedro, who was absent. Overall, there was a lot of debate but we got there in the end."
2 Reporting a proposal
In the meeting, the finance lead said: "I think we should cut the marketing budget by 15%."
💬 How does Ana report this to David using reported speech?
💡 Use a reporting verb + tense shift: "The finance lead suggested that…"
Sample answer
"The finance lead suggested that we cut the marketing budget by 15%. She said it would free up funds for the new product development."
3 Reporting a warning
The legal advisor said: "If we don't finalize the contracts this week, we'll lose the deal."
💬 How does Ana report this warning?
💡 Use "warned" or "insisted" — and remember the tense shift
Sample answer
"The legal advisor warned us that if we didn't finalize the contracts that week, we would lose the deal. She said the client had already given us a final extension."
4 Reporting an admission
One team leader said: "I have to be honest — we haven't made as much progress as we'd hoped this quarter."
💬 How does Ana report this to David?
💡 "Admitted" works perfectly here
Sample answer
"James admitted that the team hadn't made as much progress as they'd hoped that quarter. He said they were aware of the issue and were already working on a plan to catch up."
5 Reporting a question
During the meeting, someone asked: "Will the new budget apply from January or from the start of Q2?"
💬 How does Ana report the question that was asked?
💡 Reported questions use statement word order: "Someone asked whether / asked when…"
Sample answer
"Someone asked whether the new budget would apply from January or from the start of Q2. Mark said he would confirm that by email the following day."
6 Summarizing the outcome
David asks: "So, what was actually decided in the end?"
💬 How does Ana summarize the key decisions?
💡 Use reported speech for decisions + action items: "They agreed that… / It was decided that…"
Sample answer
"In the end, they agreed to reduce the marketing budget by 10% — not 15% as originally proposed. They also decided that the legal team would send the contracts that afternoon. I'll circulate the minutes to everyone later today — I'll loop you in."

Part 5 · Wrap-up
Convert It! — Direct → Reported Speech
5 min

Read each direct speech quote. Say the reported speech version out loud — then click to check.

Direct speech — said in today's meeting
"I can't attend the client call tomorrow."
Said by: Pedro
Start with: "Pedro said he…"
Reported version
"Pedro said he couldn't attend the client call the following day."  (can → could; tomorrow → the following day)
Direct speech — said in today's meeting
"We have already sent the proposal to the client."
Said by: the sales manager
Start with: "The sales manager said they…"
Reported version
"The sales manager said they had already sent the proposal to the client."  (have sent → had sent)
Direct speech — said in today's meeting
"This new approach will save us at least two weeks."
Said by: the project manager
Start with: "The project manager claimed that…"
Reported version
"The project manager claimed that that new approach would save them at least two weeks."  (will → would; this → that)
Direct speech — said in today's meeting
"Are you happy with the current timeline?"
Asked by: the chair, to the team
Start with: "The chair asked whether…"
Reported version
"The chair asked whether we were happy with the current timeline."  (are → were; yes/no question uses "whether")

If there's time · Extra Activity
Which Reporting Verb Fits Best?
5–8 min

Both options are grammatically correct — but which one best reflects the speaker's tone or meaning?

"I'm absolutely certain — this strategy will double our revenue." — She _____ the strategy would double revenue.
A — claimed
B — suggested
✅ Claimed — the speaker is very confident but unverified. "Claimed" carries a subtle hint of doubt from the reporter's side. "Suggested" is too tentative for this bold statement.
"I suppose we could try delaying it." — He _____ that they delay it.
A — suggested
B — insisted
✅ Suggested — the speaker sounds uncertain ("I suppose"), so "suggested" is softer and more accurate. "Insisted" implies strong pressure.
"Fine. I didn't prepare the slides. I should have." — She _____ that she hadn't prepared the slides.
A — mentioned
B — admitted
✅ Admitted — this is a confession. "Mentioned" is neutral and misses the reluctant, confessional tone of the original.
