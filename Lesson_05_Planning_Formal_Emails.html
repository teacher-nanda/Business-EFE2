1.  Sarah is contacting Mr. Chen for the first time.
❌ False. Sarah mentions "our conversation during last Tuesday's discovery call" — they have already spoken.
2.  The project has three phases.
✅ True. Sarah lists three phases: research and planning, development and testing, and deployment and review.
3.  The deployment phase is the longest phase in the project.
❌ False. Development and testing lasts 7 weeks (Weeks 4–10); deployment is only 2 weeks (Weeks 11–12).

✅ True. "I would be grateful if you could review the attached brief before our meeting."

Part 3 · Vocabulary
Essential Email Phrases
8 min

These are the building blocks of professional email writing — learn them, and your emails will sound natural and polished immediately.

📝 Opening & context
I am writing to…
I am writing to inform you of a change to the project schedule.
Standard formal opening — states the purpose immediately
With reference to / With regard to
With regard to your recent inquiry, I am happy to provide further details.
Links your email to a previous communication or topic
I am writing to follow up on…
I am writing to follow up on our conversation from last week regarding the contract.
Checks in on something discussed or promised previously
📝 Requests & attachments
Please find attached…
Please find attached the meeting agenda and supporting documents.
Standard phrase for pointing to an attachment — more formal than "I've attached"
I would be grateful if…
I would be grateful if you could confirm your attendance by Friday.
A polite, formal way to make a request
Please do not hesitate to…
Please do not hesitate to contact me if you have any questions.
Invites the reader to reach out — common in formal closings
📝 Closing lines
I look forward to hearing from you.
Thank you for your time. I look forward to hearing from you.
Classic formal closing — signals you expect a reply
I look forward to our meeting.
I look forward to our meeting on Thursday and to discussing this further.
Used when a meeting has already been scheduled
Kind regards / Best regards
Kind regards, Sarah Mitchell | Account Manager
Standard professional sign-off — not "Cheers" or "Best" with someone you don't know
🚀 Going further
I trust that…
I trust that this information is helpful and that you will find the proposal satisfactory.
A confident, formal assumption — implies you expect a positive outcome
Should you require…
Should you require any further information, please contact our team directly.
Formal conditional — equivalent to "If you need…" but more polished
I would like to take this opportunity to…
I would like to take this opportunity to thank you for your continued support.
Used to add a polite extra point — common in formal correspondence

Part 4 · Grammar
Upgrading Your Register
10 min

In formal writing, small choices — a contraction, a phrasal verb, a casual word — can make you sound unprofessional even if your grammar is perfect. Here's what to watch for.

📋 Three key areas to upgrade
⚡ Informal / casual
✅ Formal / professional
Contractions
"I can't make it on Friday." · "I'm writing about…"
Full forms
"I cannot attend on Friday." · "I am writing regarding…"
Phrasal verbs
"look into it" · "get back to you" · "put off the meeting"
Single-word verbs
"investigate" · "respond to you" · "postpone the meeting"
Casual words
"ASAP" · "thanks a lot" · "sorry" · "let me know"
Formal equivalents
"at your earliest convenience" · "thank you for…" · "I apologize for…" · "please do not hesitate to inform me"
4
Which version is more formal? — click the best option Grammar

Both options fix something — but only one is truly formal. Can you spot why the other still doesn't work?

⚡ Informal: "I'm writing about the meeting we had last week."
A"I am writing about the meeting we had last week."
B"I am writing with reference to our meeting last week."
A fixes the contraction ("I'm" → "I am") but keeps "about" — still too casual. B also replaces "about" with the formal phrase "with reference to".
⚡ Informal: "We need to put off the launch until next quarter."
A"We will need to postpone the launch until the following quarter."
B"We need to postpone the launch until next quarter."
B replaces the phrasal verb "put off" with "postpone" — good. But "We need to" is still abrupt and informal, and "next quarter" sounds casual. A is more measured in tone throughout.
⚡ Informal: "Let me know if you've got any questions."
A"Please do not hesitate to contact me should you have any questions."
B"Please let me know if you have any questions."
B fixes "you've got" → "you have" and adds "Please" — better, but "let me know" is still too casual for a formal email. A replaces it with the standard closing phrase.

Part 5 · Speaking
Say It Like a Pro
10 min
📌 Useful phrases from previous lessons — keep using these!
"I am writing to follow up on our conversation…" — opening a follow-up email (situations 1 & 3)
"As discussed, …" — referencing what was said or agreed in a previous call or meeting (situations 2 & 3)
"I would like to refer back to what was agreed…" — politely flagging a discrepancy without sounding accusatory (situation 2)
"Please find attached…" — introducing an attachment formally (situation 3)
"I would be happy to arrange a call at your convenience." — proposing a meeting or next step at the close of an email (situation 3)
6
The Email Dilemma — discuss & speak Speaking

Three real workplace situations. For each one: think about the situation and speak your key sentences out loud.

Situation 1 — The unpaid invoice
A client owes you payment for a project completed 45 days ago. You have emailed twice already. They've been polite but haven't paid. This is your third follow-up.
💬 Discuss & speak:
  • How do you mention the overdue payment without sounding aggressive or threatening?
  • Which phrases from today fit the opening? The request? The closing?
  • Try to say your opening line and one request sentence out loud.
Sample sentences:
Opening: "I am writing to follow up on my previous emails regarding invoice #INV-2045, now 45 days overdue."
Request: "Could you please confirm when we can expect to receive payment? We would appreciate settlement by the end of this week."
Closing: "Should you require any further information, please do not hesitate to contact me. I look forward to your prompt response."
Situation 2 — The professional 'no'
A supplier sends you a contract with pricing 30% higher than what was agreed verbally. You cannot accept these terms — but you want to keep the relationship and negotiate.
💬 Discuss & speak:
  • How do you refer back to the original agreement without sounding accusatory?
  • What is the formal way to say "this is different from what we agreed"?
  • Speak one or two key sentences from this email.
Sample sentences:
Opening: "I am writing to refer back to what was agreed during our call of [date] regarding the project pricing."
Flagging the issue: "I would like to bring to your attention that the contract received reflects a figure approximately 30% higher than the rate verbally confirmed."
Closing: "I would be happy to arrange a call at your convenience to discuss this further and reach an agreement that works for both parties."
Situation 3 — The delayed report
You promised to send a quarterly report to your manager by last Friday. It is now Monday and the report is almost ready, but you need one more day. Write a short apologetic email explaining the delay and giving a new deadline.
💬 Discuss & speak:
  • How do you open formally when apologizing for a delay?
  • What is a professional way to give a new deadline without sounding unreliable?
  • Try saying your opening, your explanation, and your closing out loud.
Sample sentences:
Opening: "I am writing to apologize for the delay in submitting the quarterly report, which was due on Friday."
Explanation & new deadline: "I require one additional day to finalize the figures and ensure accuracy. I will send the completed report by end of business tomorrow."
Closing: "I apologize for any inconvenience this may cause and appreciate your understanding. Please do not hesitate to contact me should you have any questions."

Extra · If you have time
Email Makeover
5 min

Someone wrote this email in a hurry — and it shows. Each part has at least one problem with register or phrasing.

Can you spot what's wrong before clicking to reveal the fix?

❌ Part 1 — Greeting & opening
"Hey Lisa, I'm writing to get back to you about what we talked about last week."
✅ How to fix it
1"Hey" → Dear Ms. Rivera, — formal greeting always uses title + surname, not a first name alone.
2"I'm" → I am — no contractions in formal writing.
3"get back to you about what we talked about" → I am writing to follow up on our conversation — avoid phrasal verbs; use a standard email opening phrase.
❌ Part 2 — Declining a meeting
"I can't make the Friday meeting but I'll let you know when I'm free."
✅ How to fix it
1"can't" / "I'll" / "I'm" → cannot / I will / I am — expand every contraction.
2"make the meeting" → attend the meeting — "make" is too casual; "attend" is the correct formal verb.
3"let you know when I'm free" → Please do not hesitate to contact me so we can arrange an alternative time.
❌ Part 3 — Attachment & request
"I've attached the stuff you asked for. ASAP would be great to hear back."
✅ How to fix it
1"I've attached the stuff" → Please find attached the documents you requested.
2"ASAP" → at your earliest convenience — never use abbreviations in formal email.
3"great to hear back" → I would be grateful if you could respond — use a formal request structure.
❌ Part 4 — Sign-off
"Thanks a lot, Tom"
✅ How to fix it
1"Thanks a lot" → Thank you for your time. I look forward to hearing from you. — always add a closing sentence before the sign-off.
2"Tom" → Kind regards, [Full name] · [Job title] — use a formal sign-off with full identification.
