Part 1
Warm-up
True / False / It depends
1 What do you think? Choose True, False, or It depends for each statement.
1
Small talk is only for social situations — it doesn't belong in the workplace.
Small talk is an essential part of professional life. It builds rapport, eases tension before meetings, and strengthens working relationships. It very much belongs in the workplace!
2
Asking someone about their weekend is a good way to start a Monday morning conversation.
It depends on your relationship with the person and the workplace culture. It is generally a safe topic, but in some very formal environments people prefer to go straight to business.
3
You should avoid talking about the weather — it is too boring and meaningless.
Actually, talking about the weather is a classic and perfectly acceptable conversation opener in many cultures. It is neutral, universal, and non-controversial — exactly what small talk needs to be.
4
Good small talk skills can have a positive effect on your professional reputation.
True! People who are good at small talk are often seen as approachable, confident, and socially intelligent. These qualities can help you build stronger networks and leave a great first impression.

The Art of Small Talk at Work

Small talk is the light, informal conversation that happens before a meeting begins, in the elevator, at the coffee machine, or during a lunch break. It may seem trivial, but researchers say it plays an important role in building trust and connection in the workplace.

Before remote work became so widespread, colleagues used to chat face-to-face much more often. Now, many professionals are not used to starting conversations without a screen in front of them. Getting used to making natural small talk can feel awkward at first, but the skill improves with practice and awareness.

Experts recommend keeping small talk light and positive. Safe topics include the weather, recent events, travel, food, and general work updates. Topics to avoid include salary, politics, religion, and personal relationships. The goal is to find common ground — something both people can relate to and enjoy discussing briefly.

One of the most important rules: listen as much as you speak. Asking a follow-up question shows genuine interest and keeps the conversation going naturally. And when it is time to end the conversation, a simple phrase like "It was great catching up!" or "Let's do this again soon" closes things warmly.

to start a conversation and make people feel comfortable

to talk with someone you haven't seen recently
Let's have coffee next week and catch up on everything.
bring up
to introduce a topic into a conversation
Don't bring up last year's budget issues at the client dinner.

to stay in contact with someone
It was great meeting you — let's keep in touch!

to chat with someone, often to fill a silence

Part 4
Grammar
Used to · Be used to · Get used to
1 Study the key language, then do the practice exercise below.
🔷 used to + infinitive — past habits & states
past habit We used to have weekly team meetings in person.
➜ This no longer happens. It was a regular past habit.
negative I didn't use to enjoy networking events.
➜ No "d" in questions and negatives: use to, not used to.
question Did you use to work in an open-plan office?
🔶 be used to + noun/gerund — already familiar
state I am used to working long hours.
➜ It is normal for me. I am already comfortable with it.
example She is used to the new office layout.
🔸 get used to + noun/gerund — becoming familiar
process I am slowly getting used to the new software.
➜ I am in the process of becoming comfortable with it.
example It takes time to get used to a new routine.
2 Choose the correct option to complete each sentence.
1. We _____ have a quiet open-plan office, but they hired thirty new people last month!
✅ used to — describes a past state that no longer exists. The office was quiet before; now it is not.
2. Are you _____ working from home yet, or do you still miss the office?
✅ getting used to — asks whether the process of becoming comfortable is complete. "Are you getting used to it?" means "Is it becoming normal for you?"
3. Don't worry about the noise — she _____ working in busy environments. It doesn't bother her.
✅ is used to — she is already familiar and comfortable. The process is complete. "It doesn't bother her" confirms she is already adapted.
4. He _____ travel every week for work, but now he manages everything remotely.
✅ used to — describes a past habit (weekly travel) that no longer happens. The phrase "but now" signals a change from the past.

Part 5
Speaking
3 Situations
Situation 1 of 3
Situation 1 · Reconnecting
You run into a colleague in the hallway who returned from parental leave three weeks ago. You haven't had the chance to talk. Start the conversation, ask how things are going, and mention one change at work they might not know about.
💡 Use: "Long time no see!" / "How are you finding it?" / "Things have changed a lot since you left."
"Hey, welcome back! Long time no see — how have you been? How are you finding it, being back? It must feel strange after so long at home... By the way, things have changed a bit since you left — we moved to a new project management system. You'll get used to it quickly, though. Let me know if you need any help!"
Situation 2 · Meeting a client early
You are in the conference room waiting for a meeting to start. A client arrives ten minutes early. Make small talk to fill the time before others arrive. Find some common ground and keep the conversation light.
💡 Use: "Did you have any trouble finding us?" / "How are things over at [company]?" / "Have you been to this area before?"
"Thanks for coming in! Did you have any trouble finding the office? The parking around here used to be much easier — it's gotten quite busy lately. Can I get you a coffee or some water while we wait? How are things going over at your end? I heard you recently launched a new product line — that must be keeping everyone busy!"
Situation 3 · Monday morning chat
It's Monday morning. A colleague mentions they spent the weekend moving to a new apartment. Ask two follow-up questions, share something briefly about your own weekend, and end the conversation naturally before heading to your desk.
💡 Use: "Oh wow, that's exciting!" / "How are you finding the new neighborhood?" / "Anyway, let's catch up properly later!"
"Oh wow, big weekend! How did the move go? And how are you finding the new neighborhood — is it much further from work? My weekend was pretty quiet by comparison — I just visited my family and did some cooking. Nothing exciting! Anyway, you must be exhausted. Let's catch up properly over lunch — I want to hear all about it!"
💡 Useful phrases
"How are you finding it?" — asking someone how they are adapting to something new
"Long time no see!" — greeting someone you haven't spoken to in a while
"Things have changed a lot since..." — introducing a topic about workplace changes
"Anyway, let's catch up properly soon!" — wrapping up a conversation warmly
"I'm slowly getting used to it." — talking about adapting to something new

Discussion
1 Think about and discuss the following situation.
🌐 Networking Event

You are attending a company networking event. You don't know many people there. How do you start conversations? What topics would you bring up? What would you avoid? And how would you end a conversation politely if you want to move on and talk to someone else?

Think about: opening lines · safe topics · follow-up questions · polite exits

Opening: I'd start with something simple like "Hi, I don't think we've met — I'm [name], I work in the [department] team."

Safe topics: I'd ask about their role, how long they've been with the company, or what projects they're working on. If the event has food or drinks, that's always an easy topic too!

Topics to avoid: Salary, office politics, personal relationships, or anything controversial.

Polite exit: "It's been really great talking to you — I'm going to grab another drink, but let's keep in touch! Here's my card."
