Part 1 · Warm-up
Have You Ever…?
5 min

Click Yes or No — then we'll talk about your answers.

1.  Have you ever given an approximate figure in a meeting that turned out to be far off the real number?
Giving ballpark figures is normal in meetings — but knowing how to frame them correctly ("give or take", "roughly") helps you sound confident rather than vague.
2.  Have you ever noticed that two reports showed different numbers for the same data?
A gap between two sets of data is called a discrepancy — a key word for today's lesson.
3.  Have you ever had to explain why actual results were lower than expected?
When results fall short of a target, that gap is called a shortfall. You'll also learn how to "account for" it — meaning explain why it happened.
4.  Have you ever said "a few" when you meant "not many"? Or "little" when you meant "some"?
Few and a few sound similar — but they carry opposite tones. Today you'll master the difference and use it precisely in meetings.
💬 Follow-up discussion

In your work, do you usually deal with exact figures or approximations? When is it acceptable — or even better — to use a rough estimate instead of a precise number?

Part 2 · Vocabulary
Quantities & Precision
10 min
📝 Key vocabulary
ballpark figure
I don't have the exact number — can I give you a ballpark figure for now?
an approximate estimate; not necessarily exact
shortfall
There's a shortfall of 15% — we didn't hit the target we set at the start of the quarter.
the gap between what was expected or needed and what was actually achieved
discrepancy
There's a discrepancy between the two reports — we need to find out why.
a difference or inconsistency between two sets of information
to account for
Can you account for the shortfall? The board will need an explanation before the next meeting.
to explain or justify a figure, result, or difference — why it happened
double-check
Let me double-check the number of attendees before I confirm the room.
to check something a second time to make sure it's correct
🚀 Going further
to flag
I'd like to flag a potential issue with the Q3 projections before we move on.
to draw attention to something — often a problem or discrepancy
give or take
It'll take about three weeks, give or take a few days.
approximately; allowing for a small margin of error
to cross-reference
We should cross-reference these numbers against last year's report before presenting.
to compare information from two different sources to verify accuracy

📊 The key difference — few/little vs. a few/a little
⚠️ few / little — not enough
Negative meaning — less than expected, almost none
There are few options left. (not enough to choose from)
There is little support for this idea.
Tone: problem, concern, not ideal
✅ a few / a little — some, enough
Positive meaning — some, at least something
There are a few options we can explore.
We have a little budget remaining.
Tone: neutral or optimistic
Also remember: Use few / a few with countable nouns (meetings, options, people). Use little / a little with uncountable nouns (time, budget, evidence, support).

Part 3 · Key Language
Using Quantities Precisely
10 min
🔍 Exercise 3 — Choose the correct option

Each question tests a word from today's lesson — vocabulary or grammar. Read the context carefully and click the best option.

3
Which word or phrase fits the context? A/B choice · vocabulary & grammar mixed
1.  "I don't have the final number yet, but I can give you a _____ — roughly €80,000."
✅ Ballpark figure — an approximate estimate. A "shortfall" means the gap between what was expected and what was achieved — it doesn't describe an estimate.
2.  "There is _____ evidence to support this proposal — we need more data before we can move forward."
✅ Little — "evidence" is uncountable and the context is negative ("we need more"). "Little" = almost none. "A little" would suggest some evidence exists, which doesn't match the concern.
3.  "The two reports show different totals — there seems to be a _____ we need to investigate."
✅ Discrepancy — a difference or inconsistency between two sets of information. A "shortfall" is a gap between a target and an actual result — it doesn't apply when you're comparing two sources.
4.  "There are _____ people available to help this week — most of the team is on leave."
✅ Few — "people" is countable and the tone is negative ("most of the team is on leave"). "Few" = almost none, which signals a problem. "A few" would be neutral or positive — meaning some people are available.
5.  "Before we present, let's _____ these numbers against last month's report to make sure they match."
✅ Cross-reference — to compare information from two sources to verify accuracy. "Flag" means to draw attention to something (a problem or concern) — it doesn't involve comparing sources.

Part 4 · Guided Speaking
Reporting Numbers in a Meeting
15 min

Read each situation and respond out loud. Use the Useful phrases shown — including ones from previous lessons.

📌 Useful phrases to keep using
"I'd like to give a quick update on…" — opening a topic or sharing new information
"Before we go any further…" — pausing to flag something important mid-meeting
"She confirmed / mentioned that…" — reporting what someone said (reported speech)
"According to the latest figures…" — attributing data to a source
"He warned us that…" — reporting a concern raised in a previous conversation
Situation 1 of 5
📋 Situation 1 — Giving an estimate
Your manager asks: "Do we have the final budget for this project yet?" You don't have the exact number but have a rough idea.
Useful:
ballpark figure give or take roughly approximate
💬 Say it — then check the sample
Sample answer "I don't have the final number yet — the invoice is still pending. But I can give you a ballpark figure: we're looking at roughly €42,000, give or take a few thousand. I'll confirm the exact amount by end of week."
📉 Situation 2 — Reporting a shortfall
Sales reached only 76% of the quarterly target. Your director asks you to report the result and explain it to the team.
Useful:
shortfall fell short of flag account for
💬 Say it — then check the sample
Sample answer "I need to flag something. We fell short of our quarterly target — there's a shortfall of 24%. The director confirmed that this would be discussed at Friday's board meeting, so I'll prepare a full breakdown before then."
🔎 Situation 3 — Flagging a discrepancy
You're reviewing two documents before a client presentation. The revenue figures are different in each one. You need to raise this now.
Useful:
discrepancy cross-reference double-check flag
💬 Say it — then check the sample
Sample answer "Before we move on, I'd like to flag a discrepancy. The two documents don't show the same revenue figures. Can we take a moment to cross-reference them with the original data? I want to double-check before we present anything to the client."
📊 Situation 4 — Accounting for a result
The director asks: "Can you account for the drop in customer satisfaction this quarter?" You have a few possible reasons but limited hard data.
Useful:
account for a few little observations
💬 Say it — then check the sample
Sample answer "There's little hard data at this stage, but I can offer a few observations. The new platform rolled out mid-quarter and there were technical issues in the first two weeks — that likely affected user experience. She mentioned that support tickets spiked during that period, which would account for part of the drop."
⏱️ Situation 5 — Warning the team
The project deadline is in two days. Time and budget are almost gone. You need to alert the team and keep everyone focused on what matters.
Useful:
little few all we can do running out
💬 Say it — then check the sample
Sample answer "I want to make sure everyone is aware: we have very little time and little budget left. There are few options at this stage — all we can do is focus on the critical tasks. He warned us last week that this might happen, so let's not lose any more time."

Part 5 · Wrap-up
Fix the Minutes
5 min

Each draft meeting minute below contains one mistake. Pick the corrected version.

1.  Spot the mistake
📄 Draft minutes "The supplier confirmed that delivery will begin the following Monday."
✅ A — In reported speech, "will" shifts back to "would". The tense changes to match the reporting verb "confirmed" (past).
2.  Spot the mistake
📄 Draft minutes "There were few time remaining, so the last agenda point was postponed."
✅ B — "Time" is uncountable, so it takes "little" (not "few"). Two errors fixed: "few" → "little" and "were" → "was".
3.  Spot the mistake
📄 Draft minutes "The finance team was asked to give or take the two versions of the expense report."
✅ B — "Give or take" is used with numbers to mean "approximately" (e.g. €50,000, give or take). It is not a verb meaning "compare". "Cross-reference" means to check one document against another.
4.  Spot the mistake
📄 Draft minutes "Sara mentioned that there are only a little options left and the team needs to decide quickly."
✅ A — Two fixes: "are" → "were" (reported speech backshift), and "a little" → "a few" because "options" is countable. "Needs" also shifts to "needed".
5.  Spot the mistake
📄 Draft minutes "James presented a discrepancy of around €80,000 for the total project costs."
✅ B — A "discrepancy" is an inconsistency between two different figures — not an estimate. When giving an approximate total, you give a "ballpark figure".
