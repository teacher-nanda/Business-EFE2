Part 1 · Warm-up
Jobs & Applications: What Do You Think?
5 min

Think about job applications in your professional life. Are these statements True, False, or It depends?

Reading the full job description before applying is always essential.
True. The job description tells you what the role involves, what qualifications are needed, and whether you are a good fit. Applying without reading it carefully can lead to wasted time for both sides.
A cover letter is just a summary of what's already in your resume.
False. A cover letter explains why you want the role and what makes you the right person for it. It shows your personality and motivation — things a resume alone cannot communicate.
You can apply for a job even if you don't meet every requirement listed.
It depends. If you meet most of the key requirements, it is usually worth applying. However, if you are missing several essential qualifications, your application is unlikely to be successful at this stage.
HR is always the department that makes the final hiring decision.
It depends. In many organizations, HR manages the process and screens candidates, but the final decision is made by the hiring manager in the relevant department. In smaller companies, one person may handle both.

Part 3 · Vocabulary
Key Job Application Language
8 min
1 Study these key words and phrases. Vocabulary
responsibilities
The duties and tasks that are part of a job role
"One of her main responsibilities is coordinating staff training."
qualifications
The skills, education, or experience needed for a position
"The role requires strong qualifications in HR and management."
cover letter
A formal letter sent with a resume when applying for a job
"Her cover letter explained why she was the ideal candidate."
shortlist (v)
To select the best candidates from all who applied
"Only five applicants were shortlisted for the final interview."
specialize in
To focus professional work on one particular area or field
"Their HR team specializes in talent acquisition and onboarding."
report to
To be managed by or answer directly to someone
"In this role, you will report to the Regional HR Manager."

Part 4 · Grammar
Articles & Dependent Prepositions
10 min
📌 Key Language — Articles: a / an / the / (no article)
Use "a" or "an" — for new information
She is a software developer. (new info · consonant sound)
He submitted an application. (new info · vowel sound)
We need an HR coordinator. ("H" = "aitch" · vowel sound)
Use "the" — for specific / already known information
Please complete the application form. (the one we already mentioned)
She manages the HR department. (specific department at this company)
No article — for general plurals and proper nouns
— Engineers need strong communication skills. (engineers in general)
She works at — Meridian Global. (company names)
He studied in — Japan. (most country names)
📌 Key Language — Dependent Prepositions for Job Applications
Applying and responsibility
apply for a job / a position / the role
apply to a company / an organization
responsible for managing / training / reporting
Experience and interest
experienced in recruitment / HR / finance
interested in the position / this opportunity
qualified for the job / this role
specialize in talent acquisition / HR
report to a manager / the HR Director
⚠️ Watch out: you apply for a job but apply to a company. The preposition changes depending on what follows!

Part 5 · Speaking
Talking About Jobs & Applications
10 min
📌 Useful phrases from previous lessons — keep using these!
"I'm currently responsible for..." — describing your main duties at work
"I have experience in..." — talking about your professional background
"I'm applying for the position of..." — introducing which job you want
"Could you tell me more about...?" — asking for clarification or information
"I'm very interested in this opportunity because..." — explaining your motivation
Situation 1 of 3
📋 Situation 1 — Discussing a Job Posting
You and a colleague are looking at a job posting online. You are thinking about applying for the role.
Your task: Discuss the responsibilities and requirements with your colleague. Express your interest in the role and say which requirements you meet. Ask for their opinion — should you apply?

Use: responsibilities · qualifications · apply for · experienced in · interested in
Sample: "I'm really interested in this role — the responsibilities look like a great fit. I'm already experienced in recruitment and record-keeping, and I have the right qualifications too. I think I should apply for it. What do you think — does my background match what they're looking for?"
📞 Situation 2 — Describing Your Background
A recruiter calls you for a phone screening. They ask you to briefly describe your professional background and what you are currently working on.
Your task: Introduce yourself professionally. Describe what you specialize in, what you are responsible for, and who you report to. Then explain why you are interested in the new role.

Use: specialize in · responsible for · report to · interested in · experienced in
Sample: "I've been working in HR for about three years. I specialize in recruitment and onboarding. In my current role, I report to the HR Director and I'm responsible for shortlisting candidates and coordinating interviews. I'm experienced in working with international teams, which is why I'm so interested in this opportunity — it seems like a natural next step."
✉️ Situation 3 — Asking for Cover Letter Advice
You found a job you want to apply for. You ask a more experienced colleague for advice on what to include in your cover letter and how to structure it.
Your task: Explain the job and what you plan to write. Ask your colleague for specific advice. Respond to their suggestions and decide together what the key points should be.

Use: cover letter · apply for · qualifications · responsible for · qualified for
Sample: "I'm going to apply for an HR Coordinator role and I'm not sure how to start my cover letter. Should I open by explaining my qualifications, or by saying why I'm interested in the company? — I like your suggestion to start with why I want this specific role. Then I can mention what I'm currently responsible for and show I'm qualified for the position. Does that structure work?"

Extra Practice
Real-Life Situations
Optional

Try these more challenging situations. Speak for at least 60 seconds each time.

💬 Scenario 1 — The Employment Gap
During a screening call, the recruiter notices you have a gap in your employment history and asks you to explain it.
Your task: Explain the gap confidently and professionally. Focus on what you learned or accomplished during that time. Use the phrases: responsible for, specialize in, experienced in.
Sample answer: "I took about eight months away from a formal position to complete a certification in HR management. During that time, I was also responsible for some freelance recruitment work, which meant I remained experienced in candidate assessment and shortlisting. I specialize in that area, so staying active helped me keep my skills sharp. I'm now ready to bring all of that into a full-time role."
💬 Scenario 2 — Why Are You Leaving Your Current Role?
A hiring manager asks you the classic question: "Why do you want to leave your current position?"
Your task: Give a professional, positive response. Focus on growth rather than problems. Use: specialize in, interested in, qualified for, apply for.
Sample answer: "I've learned a great deal in my current role and I'm grateful for the experience. However, I'm now looking to take on more responsibility. I'm very interested in roles where I can specialize in the full recruitment cycle — from sourcing to onboarding. I believe I'm qualified for this position, which is exactly why I decided to apply for it. It's the natural next step in my career."
