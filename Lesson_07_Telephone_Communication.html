Part 1 · Warm-up
Call or Type?
5 min

Think about phone calls in your professional life. Are these statements True, False, or It depends?

A phone call is always faster than sending an email.
It depends. For urgent or complex issues, a call can save time — but for simple updates, a quick email may be faster.
You should always identify yourself when answering a business call.
True. Saying your name and company when answering sounds professional and helps the caller confirm they've reached the right place.
Leaving a voicemail is considered unprofessional in a business context.
False. A clear, professional voicemail is perfectly acceptable — it saves the other person time and shows you made an effort to reach them.
You should always send a follow-up email after an important phone call.
It depends. For meetings, agreements, or important decisions, a follow-up email confirms what was discussed. For routine calls, it's usually not necessary.
💬 Discuss:
When do you prefer to call rather than email? What makes a phone call feel professional or unprofessional to you?

Alex:"You're welcome. Have a good morning. Goodbye."

Part 3 · Vocabulary
Telephone Expressions
8 min
1 Key Expressions Vocabulary
put on hold
Ask a caller to wait on the line while you check something or connect them.
"Could you hold the line for a moment? I'll check if she's available."
put (someone) through
Connect a caller to another person or extension.
"I'll put you through to the sales department right away."
get cut off
Lose the phone connection unexpectedly during a call.
"I'm sorry — we got cut off. Let me call you back."
call back
Return a missed call or call someone again later.
"She's away from her desk. Could she call you back this afternoon?"
take a message
Write down what a caller wants to say for someone who is not available.
"He's on another call. Would you like me to take a message?"
speak up
Talk more loudly — often requested when the line is unclear.
"I'm sorry, could you speak up? The line is a bit unclear."

Part 4 · Key Language
What to Say and When
10 min
1 Reference — Phrases by Function Vocabulary
📞 Key Telephone Language
Answering a call
"Good morning, [Company], [Name] speaking."
"Thank you for calling [Company]. How can I help you?"
Making a call
"Good morning. This is [Name] calling from [Company]."
"Could I speak to [Name / Department], please?"
Saying someone is unavailable
"I'm afraid [Name] is not available at the moment."
"She's in a meeting / on another call / away from her desk."
Taking / leaving a message
"Would you like to leave a message?"
"Could I take your name and number?"
"I'll make sure she gets your message."
Asking for clarification
"Could you repeat that, please?"
"Could you spell that for me?"
"I'm sorry, the line is a bit unclear — could you speak up?"
Ending a call
"Thank you for calling. Have a good day. Goodbye."
"I'll get back to you as soon as possible."
2 Which phrase fits? Practice

Choose the most appropriate response for each situation.

1. You pick up the phone at work. What do you say first?
A"Good morning, ConnectPro, Jamie speaking."
B"This is Jamie calling from ConnectPro."
C"Could I speak to someone in sales, please?"
A is correct — state the company and your name when answering. Option B is used when making a call.
2. The caller is speaking very quietly and you can't understand them.
A"Could you spell that for me?"
B"I'm sorry, the line is a bit unclear — could you speak up?"
C"Could you hold the line for a moment?"
B is correct — when you can't hear well, ask them to speak up. "Spell that" (A) is for unclear names/words, and "hold the line" (C) is to ask them to wait.
3. The person the caller needs is at a meeting. What do you say?
A"She doesn't want to be disturbed."
B"She's not here today."
C"I'm afraid she's in a meeting at the moment. Would you like to leave a message?"
C is correct — always explain politely why they can't be reached and offer an alternative (message or call back).
4. You want to connect the caller to a different department.
A"Could you hold the line? I'll put you through to the accounts team."
B"Could you call back and ask for accounts directly?"
C"I'll make sure she gets your message."
A is correct — ask them to hold, then transfer (put through) to the right person. Asking them to call back again (B) is unhelpful.

Part 5 · Speaking
Your Turn — Role-Play Calls
10 min
📌 Useful phrases from previous lessons — keep using these!
"As discussed, …" — referencing what was agreed or mentioned previously
"I would like to follow up on …" — opening a follow-up conversation or email
"Please find attached …" — introducing a document in a message or after a call
"If I understand correctly, …" — confirming what you heard before responding
"Could you be a bit more specific?" — asking for clearer information

Three telephone situations. Read the scenario, prepare your response, then check the sample.

Situation 1 of 3
📞 Situation 1 — Taking a Message
A caller asks to speak to your manager, who is currently away from her desk. You answer the call and handle it professionally.
Your role: Answer the call, explain that your manager is unavailable, offer to take a message, and get the caller's name, number, and reason for calling.
Sample response:
"Good morning, Brightfield Solutions, Sam speaking. How can I help you?"
[Caller asks for the manager]
"I'm afraid Ms. Torres is away from her desk at the moment. Would you like to leave a message, or would you prefer her to call you back?"
[Caller wants to leave a message]
"Of course. Could I take your name and number, please? … And may I ask what the call is regarding? … Thank you. I'll make sure she gets your message as soon as she's back."
📞 Situation 2 — Rescheduling
You need to call a client to let them know you have to postpone a meeting that was scheduled for tomorrow. You also want to suggest a new time.
Your role: Introduce yourself, explain the reason for rescheduling, apologize, and propose two alternative times.
Sample response:
"Good afternoon. This is Jamie Park calling from DataBridge Consulting. I'm calling regarding our meeting scheduled for tomorrow morning."
"Unfortunately, I need to ask if we could reschedule — something urgent has come up on our end. I sincerely apologize for any inconvenience."
"Would Thursday at 10 AM work for you, or perhaps Friday afternoon? I'm happy to work around your schedule."
📞 Situation 3 — Bad Connection
You're on a call with an important client, but the line quality is very poor — you can barely hear them and you keep missing key information.
Your role: Handle the situation professionally — ask them to repeat or speak up, confirm what you did understand, and suggest calling back on a better line if needed.
Sample response:
"I'm terribly sorry — the line is a bit unclear on my end. Could you speak up slightly?"
"I apologize, could you repeat the last part? I didn't quite catch the date you mentioned."
"Just to confirm — you said the order number is 7-7-3-2, is that right?"
"I'm so sorry, the connection seems to be cutting in and out. Would it be possible for me to call you back on a different line? I want to make sure I have all the details correct."

Extra Activity
Tricky Calls
5 min

These situations require extra care. Discuss how you would handle each one.

Situation 1 — The Frustrated Caller
A caller is angry because they have been waiting for a call back for two days. They demand to speak to a manager immediately, but no manager is available right now.
  • How do you acknowledge their frustration without agreeing that the company made a mistake?
  • What do you offer instead of putting them through to a manager?
Sample: "I completely understand your frustration, and I sincerely apologize for the delay. Unfortunately, our manager is unavailable at this moment, but I will personally ensure this is escalated as a priority. Could I take your details and have someone call you within the next two hours?"
Situation 2 — Too Much Information
A caller is giving you a lot of important details very quickly — names, dates, order numbers, and special instructions — and you're struggling to keep up.
  • How do you politely interrupt to slow them down?
  • How do you confirm you've noted everything correctly at the end?
Interrupting politely: "I'm so sorry to interrupt — could you slow down slightly? I want to make sure I get every detail right."
Confirming: "Just to make sure I have everything — the order number is 4412, delivery is needed by the 15th, and you need the expedited shipping option. Is that correct?"
