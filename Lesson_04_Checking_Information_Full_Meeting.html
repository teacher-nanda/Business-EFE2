Part 1 · Warm-up
Rate It — How Polite Is This?
5 min

Someone in the meeting just rattled off a number too fast and you missed it. Rate each response below for how polite and professional it sounds — click the stars.

"Could you repeat that, please?"
"I'm sorry — I didn't quite catch that. Would you mind saying it one more time?"
"What? I didn't hear you."
🥇
"I'm sorry — I didn't quite catch that. Would you mind saying it one more time?"
Most natural and polite. Acknowledges the issue ("I'm sorry"), names it ("didn't catch"), and softens the request ("would you mind"). You'll learn phrases like this in Part 3 today.
🥈
"Could you repeat that, please?"
Perfectly fine and professional — polite and direct. A solid default. Could feel slightly abrupt in some formal contexts, but it works well.
🥉
"What? I didn't hear you."
Understood, but too casual for a formal meeting. "What?" especially can come across as rude or impatient — even if that wasn't the intention.

Have you ever pretended to understand something in a meeting because you didn't want to ask again? What happened?
Do you think it's riskier to stay silent or to ask — and does your answer change depending on who's speaking?

Part 2 · Vocabulary
Checking & Clarifying Language
10 min
📝 Key vocabulary
That project is on track, isn't it? We're meeting the deadline, aren't we?
question tag
[a short question added to the end of a statement — used to confirm or check]
"We need to cut 20%." — "Cut 20%? Are you sure about that figure?"
echo question
[repeating part of what someone said — used to check or show surprise]
The board announced a major budget cut affecting all departments next quarter.
budget cut
[a reduction in the money available for a project or department]
🚀 Going further
"I didn't quite catch the last figure — could you say it again?"
quite
[a softener — makes a statement less direct. "Not quite" = not exactly. Not the same as "very".]
"Sorry, I didn't catch that — could you repeat the deadline?"
to catch
[to hear or understand something. "I didn't catch it" = I missed it / didn't hear it clearly]
"That's too vague — could you be more specific about the timeline?"
vague / specific
[vague = unclear, not detailed  ·  specific = exact and detailed. Opposites — know both.]
1
Fix the vocabulary — one word is wrong in each sentence Error correction
"I didn't quiet catch the last number — could you say it again?"
quiet → "I didn't quite catch that…" — quite is a softener meaning "fully / completely." Quiet means silent — a completely different word.
"His update was very specific — he just said 'things are going well.'"
specific → "His update was very vague…" — "things are going well" gives no detail, so it is vague (unclear), not specific (detailed).
"The company announced a budget increase last year, which led to layoffs across all departments."
budget increase → "…a budget cut…" — layoffs result from less money, not more. A budget cut is a reduction in available funds.

Part 3 · Key Language
Question Tags & Polite Checking
10 min
❓ Question tags — how they work
Positive statement → Negative tag
The figures are correct, aren't they?
Negative statement → Positive tag
We haven't missed the deadline, have we?
Use a pronoun — never repeat the noun
She'll send the report, won't she?
The team confirmed, didn't they?
❌ "Sarah will send it, won't Sarah?" → won't she?
Special case: I am → aren't I
I'm on the list of attendees, aren't I?

3
Error correction — one mistake per sentence
"We have few time left, so let's move to the final agenda item."
"few time" → "We have a little time left…" — "time" is uncountable, so use "little / a little". "Few" is only for countable nouns.
"She attended few meetings last quarter, which was great for team alignment."
The context is positive ("great") → "She attended a few meetings…" — "few" implies not enough (negative); "a few" = some, which fits the positive context.
"The results were good, weren't the results?"
Question tags always use a pronoun, not the full noun: "The results were good, weren't they?"
"Could you be more specifically about the timeline?"
"more specifically" → "Could you be more specific…?" — After "be", use an adjective ("specific"), not an adverb ("specifically").
⚡ Quick Recap

One final challenge — this sentence has three mistakes. Can you find all three?

"The figures are correct, weren't the figures? And we only have few time left to decide."
Three errors: (1) "weren't" → aren't — the main verb is "are" (present), so the tag must be present too. (2) "the figures" → they — tags use pronouns, not full nouns. (3) "few time" → little time — "time" is uncountable.

Part 4 · Guided Speaking
Full Meeting Simulation
20 min

You're in a live project review meeting. Respond to each situation using the language from today — and from previous lessons.

📌 Useful phrases to keep using
"She confirmed / mentioned that…" — reporting what was said in a previous meeting (situations 2 & 8)
"Before we go any further…" — pausing to flag something important mid-meeting (situation 5)
"According to the latest figures…" — introducing data or an estimate (situation 3)
"…roughly… / …give or take…" — softening a figure when you don't have the exact number (situation 3)
"Before we wrap up…" — moving into the closing phase of a meeting (situations 9 & 10)
Situation 1 of 10
Opening Situation 1 — Starting the meeting
You're chairing the meeting. Two people haven't arrived yet but you want to start. You want to confirm that everyone present received the agenda.
💬 Open the meeting and check about the agenda using a question tag.
💡 Try:opening sentencequestion tagconfirm something
Sample answer
"Right, let's get started — we have a full agenda today. You all received the agenda this morning, didn't you? Good. Let's begin with the sales update."
Update Situation 2 — Reporting what was decided last week
The director asks you to remind the team what was agreed in last week's meeting about the project timeline.
💬 Report what was said. Use reported speech to summarize at least two things that were agreed.
💡 Try:reported speechat least 2 decisionsvary your reporting verbs
Sample answer
"Of course. The team agreed that the launch date would be pushed to the 15th. It was also decided that each department would submit a progress report by Friday. The director confirmed that the budget would remain unchanged for now."
Numbers Situation 3 — Presenting figures
It's your turn to present the budget update. Final invoices aren't in yet, but you have an estimate. There's also a small gap between what was budgeted and what's been spent.
💬 Present the figures — use approximate language and flag the shortfall.
💡 Try:approximate languageflag a problemquantities vocabulary
Sample answer
"I'd like to give a quick update on the budget. We're looking at a ballpark figure of €92,000, give or take a few thousand — final invoices are still pending. I do need to flag a shortfall of around €8,000 against what was originally allocated. I'll have the exact figures confirmed by end of week."
Checking Situation 4 — Catching a missed detail
A colleague is going through a list of key dates quickly. You missed the second deadline — you heard a month but not the exact date.
💬 Politely ask them to repeat just the second deadline.
💡 Try:ask to repeatname what you missedbe polite
Sample answer
"Sorry — I didn't quite catch the second deadline. Would you mind repeating the date? I want to make sure I note it correctly."
Interruption Situation 5 — Flagging a discrepancy
The presenter is moving to the next slide. You notice the revenue figure on the slide is different from what's in your printed report — a difference of €15,000.
💬 Interrupt politely and flag the discrepancy before they move on.
💡 Try:polite interruptionquantities vocabularysuggest a solution
Sample answer
"Can I just stop you there? I'm seeing a discrepancy between the slide and my report — the revenue figure is €15,000 higher in your presentation. Could we cross-reference before we continue? I want to make sure we're working from the same data."
Clarification Situation 6 — Asking for clarification
The marketing lead says the campaign was "reasonably successful" and results were "fairly positive." You need clearer data before the board meeting.
💬 Ask for more specific information about the results.
💡 Try:ask for clarificationrefer to vague languageask a follow-up question
Sample answer
"Could you be a bit more specific about the results? 'Fairly positive' is quite vague — I'll need actual figures for the board meeting. What do you mean by 'reasonably successful'? Are we talking about reach, conversions, or revenue?"
Confirming Situation 7 — Confirming understanding
The director just announced a major restructuring plan with several changes. Before reacting, you want to confirm you understood the key point correctly.
💬 Confirm your understanding of the main proposal before responding.
💡 Try:confirm understandingrestate the proposalquestion tag
Sample answer
"If I understand correctly, you're proposing that the two teams merge into one and report to a single manager from next month — is that right? And the current team leads would both keep their roles, wouldn't they?"
Reported speech Situation 8 — Passing on information
A colleague who couldn't attend asks what the director said about the new policy. Summarize two or three key points from the meeting so far.
💬 Report the key decisions using reported speech.
💡 Try:reported speech3 key pointsdifferent reporting verbs
Sample answer
"The director said that the restructuring would begin next month. She also mentioned that all team leads would need to submit updated role descriptions by the end of the quarter. She warned that the budget for new hires had been frozen, so no new positions would be approved this year."
Wrapping up Situation 9 — Summarizing action items
The meeting is nearly over. As chair, you need to summarize the main action items and confirm who is doing what.
💬 Summarize at least three action items using reported speech and question tags to confirm.
💡 Try:closing languagereported speechquestion tag to confirm
Sample answer
"Before we wrap up, let me summarize the action items. We agreed that Ana would send the revised budget report by Friday, didn't we? And Carlos confirmed that he'd follow up with the supplier about the discrepancy. The marketing team said they'd share the full campaign data by Monday. Does that cover everything?"
Closing Situation 10 — Closing the meeting
It's the end of the meeting. Close professionally — confirm next steps, mention the next meeting, and thank everyone.
💬 Close the meeting using language from all four Module 1 lessons.
💡 Try:closing phrasemention next stepsquestion tagthank the team
Sample answer
"Right, let's wrap up — we've covered a lot today. The main actions are confirmed, and everyone knows what they need to do before Friday, don't they? The next meeting is scheduled for the 22nd. I'll circulate the minutes by end of day. Thanks everyone for a productive session."

Part 5 · Wrap-up
Quick-Fire Round
5 min

Choose A or B — trust your instinct!

1.  "The report hasn't been sent yet, _____?"
✅ A — has it. Negative statement → positive tag. "hasn't" → "has".
2.  You missed a figure. Which is the most natural phrase?
✅ B — "I didn't quite catch" is polite and professional. "What?" is too abrupt for a formal meeting.
3.  "Everyone agreed on the plan, _____?"
✅ B — didn't they. Positive statement → negative tag. "agreed" (past simple) → "didn't".
4.  The director said: "We will delay the launch." Reported correctly as:
✅ B — In reported speech, "will" shifts back to "would".
5.  The CEO proposed something complex. Before responding, you say:
✅ A — "If I understand correctly" confirms your interpretation. B is for when you missed something — here you heard it, you just want to confirm you understood.
