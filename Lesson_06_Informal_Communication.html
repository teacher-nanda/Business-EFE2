Part 1 · Warm-up
True, False, or It Depends?
5 min

Read each statement about informal communication at work. Choose your answer — then discuss with your teacher or classmates.

1.  "Using emojis in a work message always looks unprofessional." 😬
It depends. With a close colleague over chat? Totally fine. In a formal email to a client? Probably not. The key is knowing your audience and reading the relationship.
2.  "If a colleague writes to you informally, you should always reply in the same style."
It depends. Mirroring someone's tone is usually a good idea — but if a senior colleague writes to you casually, it doesn't mean you should respond like you're texting a friend. Context always matters.
3.  "A message that's too formal to a close colleague can feel cold — or even rude."
Often true. Over-formality between close colleagues can create distance or even suggest you're unhappy with them. Tone communicates a lot — not just the words.
4.  "Most communication mistakes at work happen in informal messages, not formal ones."
Often true. People are more careful when writing formally. Informal messages are written quickly — so misunderstandings, wrong tone, and missing context are much more common there.

Part 3 · Vocabulary
Phrasal Verbs for the Workplace
8 min

Phrasal verbs are very common in informal spoken and written English. Study these eight — you'll need them for the exercises below.

catch up (with)
To speak with someone about recent news or updates; to get back to the same level as others.
"Let's catch up over coffee — I haven't seen you in ages."
inseparable
sort out
To resolve, fix or organize something that is messy or unclear.
"Could you sort out the issue with the printer before the meeting?"
separable
put off
To delay or postpone something to a later time.
"We had to put off the launch because of the technical issues."
separable
come up with
To think of or produce an idea, plan or solution.
"The team came up with three proposals for the client."
inseparable
follow up (on)
To take further action on something, or check whether something has been done.
"I'll follow up on the email I sent last week."
separable
set up
To arrange, organize or create something — often a meeting, call or system.
"Can you set up a call for Thursday afternoon?"
separable
fill in (for)
To temporarily replace or cover for someone who is absent.
"Anna filled in for the manager during his holiday."
separable
hand over
To pass responsibility, ownership or control of something to someone else.
"She handed over the project files to the new coordinator."
separable

Part 4 · Grammar
Separable vs Inseparable Phrasal Verbs
10 min
📐 Key rule — where does the object go?
Separable — noun object
With a noun, the object can go after the particle OR between the verb and particle — both are correct.
"Could you sort out the problem?" ✓
"Could you sort the problem out?" ✓
With a pronoun, use the middle position only:
"Sort it out." ✓  |  "Sort out it." ✗
Inseparable — always together
The object always comes after the full phrasal verb. You cannot split it.
"She came up with a great idea." ✓
"Let's catch up with the team." ✓
Never split inseparable verbs:
"She came a great idea up with." ✗
4 Which sentence is correct? Choose A or B. Grammar
You want to tell your manager you've taken action on a report
A"I'll follow up it tomorrow morning."
B"I'll follow it up tomorrow morning."
With pronouns and separable phrasal verbs, the pronoun always goes between the verb and the particle.
You want to say you organized the meeting
A"I've already set up the meeting for Thursday."
B"I've already set up it for Thursday."
With a pronoun, the object must go between the verb and particle: "set it up". With a noun, both positions are correct.
You want to describe how your team thought of a new plan
A"The team finally came up with a solution."
B"The team finally came with a solution up."
"Come up with" is inseparable — the object always goes after the complete phrasal verb. It can never be split.

Part 5 · Speaking
Quick Catch-up — Office Situations
10 min
📌 Useful phrases from previous lessons — keep using these!
Vocabulary"As discussed, …" — referencing something already agreed or mentioned
Vocabulary"She mentioned that…" / "He said that…" — reporting what a colleague told you
Reading"As per my last message, …" — referring back to something said in a previous written communication
Speaking"I didn't quite catch that — could you say it again?" — asking for repetition politely
Speaking"If I understand correctly, …" — confirming what you heard before responding

A colleague or manager speaks to you. Read the situation, then respond using the phrasal verbs from today's lesson. Think about your answer first, then check the sample.

Situation 1 of 5
Situation 1 — Confirming the call
A colleague sends you a message: "Hey! Are we still on for the team update call tomorrow? Just wanted to confirm."
Your turn: Confirm the call and mention you'll send a calendar invite. Use at least one phrasal verb from today.
💡 Sample: "Yes, we're still on! I'll follow it up with a calendar invite this afternoon so everyone has the details."
Situation 2 — Covering for a colleague
Your manager says: "Tom is out sick today. Could you fill in for him at the 2pm client call? It's quite important."
Your turn: Agree to fill in and ask for the necessary materials. Use at least two phrasal verbs from today.
💡 Sample: "Of course, happy to fill in. Could Tom hand over his notes before the call? I'll sort out any preparation I need from there."
Situation 3 — Brainstorming with the team
A colleague says: "We've been struggling to find a good theme for the annual team event. Any ideas?"
Your turn: Offer a couple of ideas and suggest setting up a meeting to discuss them. Use at least two phrasal verbs.
💡 Sample: "I've actually come up with a few ideas — how about a workshop or a team challenge? Let's set up a short call to go through them together."
Situation 4 — Explaining a delay
Your manager asks: "The product launch keeps getting pushed back. What's the situation right now?"
Your turn: Explain why the launch was delayed and what the team is doing about it. Use at least two phrasal verbs.
💡 Sample: "We had to put it off because of a technical issue with the platform. But the team has come up with a solution and we're sorting it out this week."
Situation 5 — Chasing a response
A colleague asks: "Did you ever hear back from the supplier about the delivery date? We've been waiting for days."
Your turn: Explain that you haven't heard back yet and say what you'll do next. Use at least two phrasal verbs.
💡 Sample: "Not yet — I'll follow it up today and push for a confirmation. If they can't sort it out, we'll need to come up with a backup option."
